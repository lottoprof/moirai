## Start Earlier Than You Think

> The most common mistake: starting the budget too late

### Your First Budget
- Your first budget is a **maximum estimate**.
- Put down the highest reasonable price for everything.
- This is not a shopping list — it's a **map of risk**.
- Once you have the ceiling, you work down from it.

---

## Hidden Costs

> The ones that always get forgotten

### Production
- Extra hours
- Equipment damage deposits
- Food for the crew every shoot day
- Transportation between locations

### Logistics
- Hard drives for daily backups
- Permits for public spaces
- Duplicates and replacements for any prop used in a scene

### Post-Production
- Editing, color grading, sound design
- These happen **after** the shoot
- Most beginners forget them until after the money is spent

---

## The Reserve

> **15%** of your total budget must be reserved for emergencies

### Know Both Numbers Before You Talk to Anyone

**Ideal Budget**
— What would it cost to make this film exactly as you imagined?  
— Every person you want, every piece of gear you need.

**Minimum Viable**
— What's the lowest cost where the film still works?  
— Not cutting everything — being specific about what's truly essential.  
— The gap between them is your **negotiating room**.

---

## From Practice

**The Reserve That Saved the Film**

Third day of a four-day shoot. Main location called — heating failure. Winter shoot, interior scenes. We couldn't use the space.  
New location found in four hours. Cost: $400 — twice the original budget.  
Because we had a 15% reserve, we covered it. Film finished on schedule.

> The reserve isn't money you plan to spend. It's money you need to have **available**.

---

## Paying People

> If you invite people to work — you pay them.

- On time. Without excuses.
- Decide the schedule **before** production starts.
- Announce it clearly. Follow through without exception.

> Late payment permanently damages trust.

## Practice (10 min)

**Budget Breakdown Exercise**

**Scenario:** one-day shoot — two actors, one interior location, crew of four.

1. Build a budget: location, equipment, crew, food for six, transportation.
2. Add one contingency item — something that could realistically go wrong.
3. Add your 15% reserve on top. Compare totals with the group.
