# Editing 1–2: The 10 Principles of Editing

## The 10 Principles of Video Editing (Alexei Sokolov)

1. **By shot size** — Shots of different sizes should be connected gradually, without abrupt jumps. For example, a long shot is followed by a medium shot, which is then followed by a close-up or detail shot.

2. **By spatial orientation** — When filming two interacting objects, the camera must remain on one side of the line of interaction throughout (see: the 180-degree rule).

3. **By direction of movement** — If a subject was moving to the right, it should not abruptly move to the left in the next shot. Any change in direction must be shown through an intermediate shot.

4. **By phase of movement** — When a continuous action is filmed across multiple shots, the phases of movement must be maintained in sequence so that the action reads as unbroken.

5. **By pace of movement** — When changing shot size, the camera angle and position must also change; otherwise the subject's speed will appear distorted on screen.

6. **By composition** — The centre of attention should not shift by more than one third of the frame between adjacent shots, or the audience will lose track of the subject.

7. **By light** — The direction and intensity of light, as well as the position of shadows, must match between cuts.

8. **By colour** — If a shot is almost entirely filled with a single colour, that colour should occupy approximately one third of the preceding shot.

9. **By camera axis** — The shooting axis and centre of attention should shift gradually, without sudden jumps.

10. **By direction of moving mass** — The direction and speed of a moving mass should match between adjacent shots. When cutting to a completely static shot, the moving mass should occupy less than one third of the frame.
