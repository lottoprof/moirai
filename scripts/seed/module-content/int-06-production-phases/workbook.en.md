# Directing as a NON-Creative Profession
**Moirai — Keep this document**

---

## LECTURE SUMMARY

The director is a generalist. Understands each department well enough to speak their language and notice when something is wrong.

The film is made three times: when you write it, when you shoot it, when you edit it.

Pre-production for a short film: minimum 6 weeks. Post-production includes editing, color grading, and sound design — budget and plan for it in advance, not after.

---

## KEY CONCEPTS

**Generalist** — a director who knows enough about every department.

**Three versions of the film** — script, shoot, edit. Each is a different film.

**Call sheet** — mandatory document sent the night before every shooting day.

---

## REAL EXAMPLE — CALL SHEET

Standard format used in professional productions:

```
CALL SHEET · [Project Title] · Day [N]

Shoot date:      [Day, Date]
General call:    [Time]
Crew on set:     [Time — usually 30 min before general call]

LOCATION:
Address:         [Full address]
Parking:         [Instructions]

SCHEDULE:
07:00  Crew arrives, equipment setup
08:00  Actors arrive, hair/makeup/costume
09:00  Blocking — Scene 7
09:30  Shoot Scene 7 (EXT. STREET — MORNING)
       Estimated shots: 8
12:00  Lunch break (1 hour — mandatory)
13:00  Move to second location
14:00  Shoot Scene 12 (INT. APARTMENT — DAY)
17:00  Shoot Scene 13
19:00  End of shoot day (planned)

CAST:
Character A   [Actor name]     Call: 08:00
Character B   [Actor name]     Call: 10:00
Character C   [Actor name]     Call: 13:00

TECHNICAL NOTES:
· Scene 7: natural light only
· Scene 7 props: 3 copies of the letter (props dept.)
· Scene 12: blackout paper needed on windows

CONTACTS:
Director:    [Name] · [Phone]
First AD:    [Name] · [Phone]
DP:          [Name] · [Phone]
Props:       [Name] · [Phone]

Next shoot day: [Date] · Location: [Address]
```

## REAL EXAMPLE — PRE-PRODUCTION PLAN

```
PROJECT: [Title] · Runtime: [min] · Shoot days: [N]

WEEK 1 — CONCEPT AND SCRIPT:
□  Lock the concept (one sentence)
□  Write first draft
□  External read: minimum 2 people
□  Revisions based on feedback
Risk: script doesn't work → have a backup concept ready

WEEK 2 — CASTING AND LOCATIONS (simultaneously):
□  Post casting call (Actors Access / local Facebook groups)
□  Accept self-tapes
□  Run in-person auditions
□  Confirm cast + hold alternates
□  Scout: minimum 3 options per location
□  Sign rental agreement
□  Confirm backup location
Risk: actor drops out → alternate confirmed in advance

WEEK 3 — DOCUMENTS AND PREP:
□  Director's script (all scenes)
□  Storyboards for key scenes
□  Shot list for each shoot day
□  Meeting with DP: lighting and equipment plan
□  Props: list + duplicates ordered
□  Costumes: fitting + photograph each outfit

WEEK 4 — FINAL CONFIRMATIONS:
□  Read-through with actors at the location
□  All contracts signed
□  Payment sheet created and sent
□  W-9 forms collected
□  Call sheet template ready
□  Backup equipment confirmed

WEEK 5 — SHOOT
```

## GLOSSARY

**Call Sheet** — daily production document sent the night before a shoot. Contains location, schedule, cast and crew, and technical notes.

**First AD** — First Assistant Director. Manages the schedule and daily logistics.

**ADR** — Automated Dialogue Replacement. Re-recording lines in a studio after the shoot.

**Color Grade** — post-production color process. Technical correction fixes defects; grading creates mood.

**Above / Below the Line** — above: director, producer, actors. Below: technical crew, equipment, locations, post-production.
