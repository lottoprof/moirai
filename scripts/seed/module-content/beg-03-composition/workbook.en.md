# Directing 3: Frame Composition

## The Six Frame Sizes (Kuleshov)

![Six Frame Sizes](Six_Frame_Sizes.png)

1. **Extreme Long Shot** — The human figure is extremely small, occupying no more than 1/7 of the frame. Emphasis is on the location; commonly used for establishing shots.
2. **Long Shot** — The full human figure fits within the frame, with a small amount of space above the head and below the feet.
3. **Medium Shot** — The figure is framed from the knees up. Works well when characters are moving through space.
4. **Medium Close-Up Shot** — The figure is framed just above the waist. Ideal for dialogue scenes between characters.
5. **Close-Up Shot** — The head (sometimes head and shoulders) fills the frame. Most commonly used to capture important emotional reactions.
6. **Extreme Close-Up / Detail Shot** — A single eye or comparably sized detail fills the frame.

---

## Rule of Thirds

![Rule of Thirds](Thirds.png)

The frame is divided into a 3×3 grid. Placing the subject at one of the four intersection points — rather than dead centre — creates a more dynamic and visually balanced composition. The horizon line is typically aligned with one of the two horizontal grid lines.

---

## Horizon Line

### High Horizon
![High Horizon](Horizon_high_.png)

The horizon is placed in the upper third of the frame. More weight is given to the foreground — the ground, the character, the immediate environment. Creates a sense of closeness or confinement.

### Low Horizon
![Low Horizon](Horizon__low_.png)

The horizon is placed in the lower third of the frame. More weight is given to the sky or background. Can suggest openness, freedom, or the character's insignificance against a larger world.

---

## Dutch Angle

![Dutch Angle](Dutch_Angle.png)

The camera is tilted on its axis so that the horizon line runs diagonally across the frame. Used to convey instability, tension, unease, or a distorted state of mind.

---

## Symmetry

![Symmetry](Simmetry.png)

The frame is composed so that both halves mirror each other along a central axis. Creates a sense of order, control, or grandeur — and can make a single figure within a vast symmetrical space feel isolated or overwhelmed.
