# Directing 6: Dialogue

## What is Dialogue?

Dialogue is a conversation between two or more characters. Each character uses their lines to interact with the other, responding to what has been said.

---

## Types of Dialogue

1. **Expository** — Used to convey information the audience needs in order to understand the plot and the characters.
2. **Functional** — Used to advance the plot; shifts the direction of the story and has a direct impact on the characters.
3. **Atmospheric** — Does not advance the plot, but reveals the inner world of the characters, introducing the audience to their personalities, behavioural patterns, likes, dislikes, etc.

> Note: dialogue types are not mutually exclusive. A single scene can combine several functions — for example, an expository dialogue may also serve an atmospheric purpose.

---

## Examples

1. [Casablanca — Major Strasser Questions Rick](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QzQzVUCCxmA) — A classic example of expository dialogue.

## Shooting Dialogue

### Shot-Reverse Shot
A technique for filming dialogue in which the characters alternate on screen. Each is filmed as though looking at the other, their eyelines directed toward opposite sides of the frame.

### The 180-Degree Rule
An imaginary line drawn through two interacting characters. The camera must remain on one side of this line throughout the entire scene. If the camera crosses it, the characters will appear to have swapped positions on screen, and the audience will lose their spatial orientation.

In a dialogue scene: if character A looks to the right, character B must look to the left — and this relationship must be maintained across every shot in the scene.
